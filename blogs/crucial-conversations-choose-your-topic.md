# Crucial Conversations: Choose Your Topic

**Published:** March 23, 2026

You have finally worked up the nerve to have the difficult conversation. You sit down, you start talking, and twenty minutes later the discussion has gone sideways. Emotions have escalated, you are arguing about something tangential, and the core issue remains unaddressed. This is one of the most common failure modes in crucial conversations, and it happens because people solve the wrong problem. They have the wrong conversation.

Chapter 3 of "Crucial Conversations" addresses a deceptively simple idea: before you can have a productive crucial conversation, you need to make sure you are talking about the right thing.

## Why Engineers Often Have the Wrong Conversation

Engineering culture rewards precision, but that precision often applies only to technical problems. When it comes to interpersonal issues, engineers frequently default to discussing the most recent, most concrete, or most comfortable version of a problem rather than the real one.

Consider this scenario: a teammate has missed a deployment deadline for the third time. You pull them aside and say, "Hey, we missed the deploy window yesterday. Can you make sure it does not happen again?" On the surface, this seems like a reasonable conversation. But if this is the third occurrence, the real issue is not what happened yesterday. The real issue is the pattern, or possibly what the pattern says about trust and reliability. By addressing only the surface-level event, you are having the wrong conversation, and you will almost certainly be having it again in two weeks.

Two natural tendencies pull us toward the wrong topic:

**We choose easy over hard.** It is easier to talk about a specific incident than to talk about a pattern of behavior. It is much easier to talk about a pattern than to talk about how that pattern is affecting your trust in someone. We gravitate toward the version of the conversation that feels least threatening.

**We choose recent over right.** The most recent occurrence is vivid and top of mind, so we talk about that. But the most recent event is often just a symptom. The underlying issue -- the one that would actually solve the problem if addressed -- may stretch back months.

## Three Warning Signs You Are on the Wrong Topic

The book identifies three reliable indicators that you are discussing the wrong thing:

**Emotions escalate unexpectedly.** If a conversation that should be straightforward suddenly becomes heated, it is often because the real issue is lurking beneath the stated one. You think you are talking about a late pull request, but the other person hears an attack on their competence. The emotional intensity does not match the stated topic because the actual topic is something bigger.

**You walk away skeptical.** After the conversation, you find yourself thinking "Sure, they agreed, but will anything actually change?" This skepticism is a signal that you addressed a symptom but not the cause. You talked about the what but not the why or the what-it-means.

**Deja vu.** You have had this exact conversation before. Same person, same issue, same assurances. If you are having the same discussion for the second or third time, you are definitively having the wrong conversation. Repeating the same conversation and expecting different results is a reliable sign that you need to move to a deeper level.

## The CPR Framework

The most actionable tool in this chapter is the CPR model, which gives you three distinct levels at which to engage a problem: Content, Pattern, and Relationship.

### Content: What Happened

Content-level conversations address a single instance of a problem. This is the first time something has occurred, or the first time you are raising it.

In engineering, content-level conversations sound like:

- "The API endpoint you deployed yesterday is returning 500 errors on edge cases. Can we look at the test coverage?"
- "In yesterday's design review, I noticed some of the requirements were not addressed in your proposal."
- "The on-call handoff last night was missing the context about the ongoing database migration."

Content conversations are appropriate when something happens for the first time and there is no reason to believe it reflects a deeper issue. They are specific, factual, and focused on one event.

### Pattern: What Keeps Happening

Pattern-level conversations address recurring behavior. The individual instance may be minor, but the repetition is the real problem.

In engineering, pattern-level conversations sound like:

- "This is the third sprint in a row where your stories have carried over. I want to understand what is going on."
- "I have noticed that in the last several code reviews, the feedback I have left has not been addressed before merging. This is becoming a pattern."
- "We have had multiple incidents now where the monitoring gaps were known beforehand but not prioritized. I want to talk about why this keeps happening."

The shift from content to pattern is critical. Many engineers stay stuck at the content level, addressing each occurrence as if it were isolated, and then feel frustrated when the behavior continues. If you have already had the content conversation and the problem recurs, escalating to the pattern level is necessary.

### Relationship: What This Means for Us

Relationship-level conversations address what the pattern of behavior is doing to trust, respect, or confidence between the people involved. This is the deepest and most difficult level.

In engineering, relationship-level conversations sound like:

- "When commitments are repeatedly missed without early communication, it affects my ability to trust estimates from you, and that changes how I plan the team's work."
- "The pattern of merging code without addressing review feedback is making me question whether you value the review process, and it is affecting how I approach reviewing your work."
- "We have talked about on-call handoff quality several times now. At this point, the issue is not just about handoffs -- it is about whether I can count on you to follow through on things we have agreed on."

Relationship conversations are uncomfortable because they name the impact on trust and respect directly. But they are often the only conversations that produce lasting change, because they make the real stakes visible.

## Unbundle, Choose, Simplify

The book offers a three-step process for ensuring you have the right conversation:

**Unbundle.** Before you start the conversation, identify all the different issues tangled together. A single frustrating situation often contains multiple distinct problems. A teammate's missed deadline might involve: the specific impact on the release (content), the recurring nature of missed deadlines (pattern), the lack of proactive communication about risks (a separate pattern), and the erosion of trust (relationship). These are different conversations, and trying to have all of them at once will fail.

**Choose.** From your unbundled list, select the one issue that matters most. This requires honesty with yourself about what is actually bothering you. Often, the thing you are most tempted to discuss (the specific incident) is not the thing that matters most (the pattern or the relationship impact).

**Simplify.** Distill your chosen topic into a clear, concise statement. If you cannot explain in one or two sentences what you want to discuss, you are either still bundling multiple issues or you have not identified the real topic. "I want to talk about the pattern of missed deadlines and what we can do to change it" is a clear topic. "I want to talk about a bunch of things that have been bothering me" is not.

## Putting CPR to Work

The CPR model is especially useful in engineering because technical work generates a constant stream of small issues, and deciding which level to address them at determines whether they get resolved or fester.

A useful heuristic: if it is the first time, have the content conversation. If it has happened before and you have already addressed it, move to pattern. If the pattern is affecting your working relationship, move to relationship. Do not skip levels prematurely -- jumping to a relationship conversation the first time something happens will feel disproportionate. But do not stay stuck at content when the evidence clearly points to a pattern.

The discipline of choosing your topic before entering the conversation is one of the highest-leverage habits you can develop. It takes two minutes of reflection and saves twenty minutes of unproductive discussion.

## Conclusion

Most crucial conversations fail not because people lack courage or skill, but because they are having the wrong conversation. The natural tendency to choose easy over hard and recent over right keeps people stuck at the surface level. The three warning signs -- unexpected emotional escalation, post-conversation skepticism, and deja vu -- reliably indicate that you need to go deeper. The CPR framework (Content, Pattern, Relationship) gives you a clear model for identifying the right level. And the discipline of unbundling, choosing, and simplifying your topic before you start talking is one of the simplest and most effective practices for making crucial conversations productive.
